In an unfortunate end to his prolific career, Turing (a man who should have been a&nbsp;<strong>hero of the free world</strong>&nbsp;and idolized next to&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Galileo</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Newton</a>&nbsp;in the&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>history</strong></a>&nbsp;books) was arrested in 1952 after British authorities found out he was having a relationship with another man. Under&nbsp;<strong>British law</strong>, homosexuality was a&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crime" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">crime</a>, and it resulted in Turing losing his security clearance to continue his work at&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bletchley_Park" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Bletchley Park</a>&nbsp;. Rather than&nbsp;<strong>face a life in prison</strong>, Turing the one who laid down the mathematical foundation for all computer science) accepted treatment of regular&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Estrogen" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">estrogen</a>&nbsp;injections, which were believed to neutralize&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Libido" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">libido</a>. On 8 June 1954, Turing one of the world's greatest geniuses and a man ahead of his time) died of&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_cyanide" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">potassium cyanide&nbsp;</a>poisoning while conducting&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrolysis" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">electrolysis</a>&nbsp;experiments. The&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanide" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">cyanide</a>&nbsp;was found on a half eaten apple beside him. An inquest concluded that it was self administered but his mother always maintained that it was an accident. The&nbsp;<strong>inventor of the modern digital computer</strong>&nbsp;was lost forever, and one of the greatest minds in the history of computing died in shame and disgrace. His insight was brilliant, and, beyond his contributions to the idea of the&nbsp;<strong>modern computer and artificial intelligence</strong>, he was a hero who came to a tragic end. In 2013, a bill was passed offering statutory&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pardon" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">pardon</a>&nbsp;to Turing for offences under section 11 of the&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Criminal_Law_Amendment_Act" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Criminal Law Amendment Act&nbsp;</a>1885. <p><strong>WHO WAS HE?</strong></p>
<p>________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________</p>
<p><strong>Alan Mathison Turing</strong>&nbsp;OBE FRS ( 23 June 1912 -- 7 June 1954 often called the father of modern&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computing" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">computing</a>&nbsp;) was a brilliant&nbsp;<strong>English mathematician</strong>, computer scientist, logician,&nbsp;<strong>cryptanalyst</strong>, philosopher and theoretical&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biologist" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">biologist</a>. Turing (considered one of the&nbsp;<strong>20th century's most important people</strong>) was highly influential in the development of theoretical&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_science" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">computer science</a>, providing a formalization of the idea of the modern computer and artificial intelligence and concepts of&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algorithm" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>algorithm</strong></a>&nbsp;and computation with the&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turing_machine" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Turing machine</a>, which can be considered a model of a general purpose computer. British mathematician and author&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andrew_Hodges" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>Andrew Hodges</strong></a>&nbsp;describes the significance of Turing machine in this way:</p>
<p><em>"It is now almost impossible to read Turing's 1936 work without thinking of a Turing machine as a computer program, and the Universal Turing Machine as the computer on which different programs can be run. We are now so familiar with the idea of the computer as a fixed piece of hardware, requiring only fresh software to make it do entirely different things, that it is hard to imagine the world without it. It was also essential to Turing&rsquo;s 1936 work that a Turing machine could be thought of as data to be read and manipulated by another Turing machine this is the principle of the modifiable stored program on which all computing now depends."</em></p>
<p>Turing (a celebrated twentieth century&nbsp;<strong>British mathematician</strong>&nbsp;) is widely considered to be the father of theoretical computer science and artificial intelligence. Despite these accomplishments, the story of Alan Turing is one of the most&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disgraceful" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>disgraceful&nbsp;</strong></a>episodes of modern&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civilization" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">civilization</a>. A man who should have been a code breaking hero of the free world and idolized next to&nbsp;<strong>Galileo</strong>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<strong>Newton</strong>&nbsp;in the history books was instead hounded to a permanent cessation of all vital functions because of&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religion" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">religion</a>&nbsp;inspired&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homophobia" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">homophobia</a>. Alan Turing was not a well known figure during his lifetime. But today he is famous for being an&nbsp;<strong>eccentric</strong>&nbsp;yet&nbsp;<strong>passionate Brilliant mathematician</strong>, who conceived modern computing and played a crucial part in the&nbsp;<strong>Allied victory</strong>&nbsp;over Nazi&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germany" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Germany</a>&nbsp;in&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">WW2</a>.</p>
<p>During the Second World War, Turing worked for the&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_Communications_Headquarters#Government_Code_and_Cypher_School" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>Government Code and Cypher School</strong></a>&nbsp;(GC &amp; CS) at Bletchley Park, Britain's code-breaking centre that produced Ultra intelligence. For a time he led&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hut_8" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Hut 8</a>, the section that was responsible for&nbsp;<strong>German naval&nbsp;</strong><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryptanalysis" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>cryptanalysis</strong></a>. Here, he devised a number of techniques for speeding the breaking of German ciphers, including improvements to the pre-war&nbsp;<strong>Polish</strong>&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bombe" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">bombe</a>&nbsp;method, an&nbsp;<strong>electromechanical&nbsp;</strong><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Machine" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>machine</strong></a>&nbsp;that could find settings for the&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enigma_machine" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Enigma machine</a>. Turing played a pivotal role in cracking intercepted coded messages that enabled the&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alliance" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>Allies</strong></a>&nbsp;to defeat the Nazis in many crucial engagements, including the&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_the_Atlantic" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Battle of the Atlantic</a>, and in so doing helped win the war.<strong>&nbsp;Counterfactual history</strong>&nbsp;is difficult with respect to the effect Ultra&nbsp;<strong>intelligence</strong>&nbsp;had on the length of the war, but at the upper end it has been estimated that this work shortened the war in&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europe" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Europe</a>&nbsp;by more than two years and saved over 14 million lives.</p>
<p>After the war, Turing worked at the&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Physical_Laboratory_(United_Kingdom)" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">National Physical Laboratory</a>&nbsp;, where he designed the&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automatic_Computing_Engine" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>Automatic Computing Engine</strong></a>&nbsp;(a British early electronic stored program computer), which was one of the first designs for a stored program computer. In 1948, Turing joined&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computing_Machine_Laboratory" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Max Newman's Computing Machine Laboratory</a>&nbsp;at the&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Victoria_University_of_Manchester" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Victoria University of Manchester</a>&nbsp;, where he helped develop the&nbsp;<strong>Manchester computers</strong>&nbsp;and became interested in mathematical biology. He wrote a paper on&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Chemical_Basis_of_Morphogenesis" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">the&nbsp;<strong>chemical basis of morphogenesis</strong></a>&nbsp;(which served as a basic model in theoretical biology and suggested that a system of chemical substances, called&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphogen" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">morphogen</a>s , reacting together and diffusing through a tissue, is adequate to account for the main phenomena of&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphogenesis" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">morphogenesis</a>&nbsp;) and predicted&nbsp;<strong>oscillating chemical reactions</strong>&nbsp;such as the&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belousov%E2%80%93Zhabotinsky_reaction" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>Belousov Zhabotinsky reaction&nbsp;</strong></a>(which is one of a class of reactions that serve as a classical example of&nbsp;<strong>non-equilibrium thermodynamics</strong>, resulting in the establishment of a nonlinear chemical oscillator ), first observed in the 1960s.</p>
<p>However, in early 1952-- after investigating a&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burglary" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>burglary&nbsp;</strong></a>at his home, police eventually noticed that Turing had a&nbsp;<strong>homosexual</strong>&nbsp;relationship with a 19 year old man. Police arrested Turing and he (who literally saved the world from&nbsp;<strong>Nazi domination</strong>) was prosecuted for homosexual acts; the&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Labouchere_Amendment" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Labouchere Amendment</a>&nbsp;had mandated that "<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gross_indecency" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">gross indecency</a>" was a criminal offence in the UK. The judge offered him a choice: spend one year behind bars or endure an experimental&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hormone" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">hormone</a>&nbsp;treatment to "<strong>fix</strong>" his&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sexual_orientation" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">sexual orientation</a>. Alan chose&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_castration" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>chemical castration</strong></a>&nbsp;treatment, with&nbsp;<strong>DES</strong>&nbsp;(<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diethylstilbestrol" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>Diethylstilbestrol</strong></a>&nbsp;), as an alternative to prison. Turing (a victim of the&nbsp;<strong>discriminatory laws of the day</strong>) died in 1954, 16 days before his 42nd birthday, from cyanide poisoning . An inquest determined his death as a suicide, but it has been noted that the known evidence is also consistent with&nbsp;<strong>accidental poisoning</strong>. He sadly died too young, in&nbsp;<strong>tragic circumstances</strong>, a victim of the discriminatory laws of the day. It is often wondered how much further ahead computing would be, if he had lived. The 20th century had lost one its smartest people.</p>
<p>It took decades for Turing (who was a victim of mid 20th Century attitudes to&nbsp;<strong>homosexuality&nbsp;</strong>and who was chemically castrated before dying at the age of 41 ) to receive the respect in death that he never received in life.&nbsp;<strong>On September 10, 2009</strong>&nbsp;, following an Internet campaign and high profile support from public figures such as&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stephen_Fry" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Stephen Fry</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Dawkins" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Richard Dawkins</a>&nbsp;, British Prime Minister&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gordon_Brown" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>Gordon Brown</strong></a>&nbsp;issued a public&nbsp;<strong>apology</strong>&nbsp;to Turing's memory on behalf of the British government for "the appalling way he was treated":</p>
<p><em>"Thousands of people have come together to demand justice for Alan Turing and recognition of the appalling way he was treated. While Turing was dealt with under the law of the time and we can&rsquo;t put the clock back, his treatment was of course utterly unfair and I am pleased to have the chance to say how deeply sorry I and we all are for what happened to him &hellip; So on behalf of the British government, and all those who live freely thanks to Alan&rsquo;s work I am very proud to say: we&rsquo;re sorry, you deserved so much better."</em></p>
<p>Queen&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elizabeth_II" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>Elizabeth II&nbsp;</strong></a>granted Turing a posthumous pardon in 2013. The&nbsp;<strong>Alan Turing law</strong>&nbsp;is now an informal term for a 2017 law in the United Kingdom that retroactively pardoned man cautioned or convicted under historical legislation that outlawed homosexual acts.&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Codebreaker_(film)" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>Codebreaker</strong></a>, a feature-length drama documentary aired on 21 November 2011 by Channel 4 about the life of Alan Turing, examined&nbsp;<strong>Turing's extraordinary life</strong>, tragic death, and enduring legacy. Despite the Turing's role in shaping our&nbsp;<strong>modern world</strong>, most people were unaware of his story. This film helped change that fact. Despite his brilliance, what happened to Alan Turing is one of&nbsp;<strong>history's great tragedies</strong>. When&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_(magazine)" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Time magazine</a>published its list of the 100 most important people of the twentieth century, they included Alan Turing in that list and said of him:</p>
<p><em>"The fact remains that everyone who taps at a keyboard, opening a spreadsheet or a word-processing program, is working on an incarnation of a Turing machine."</em></p>
<p>Of his many important papers, the most well known are:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>On Computable Numbers, with an Application to the&nbsp;</strong><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entscheidungsproblem" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>Entscheidungsproblem</strong></a>&nbsp;(28 May 1936) in which Alan Turing first outlined his idea of a&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_Turing_machine" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Universal Machine</a>&nbsp;which could tackle any mathematical problem and which became known as the Turing Machine.</li>
<li><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computing_Machinery_and_Intelligence" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>Computing Machinery and Intelligence&nbsp;</strong></a>(1950) in which Alan Turing first proposes the Turing test to decide whether a computer can be called intelligent or not.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Childhood &amp; Early Life</strong></p>
<p>________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________</p>
<p>Two years before the outbreak of the&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>First World War</strong></a><strong>&nbsp;</strong><em>&mdash;&nbsp;</em>Alan Mathison Turing (who was the first to conceive of the fundamental principle of the&nbsp;<strong>modern computer&nbsp;</strong>&mdash; the idea of controlling a computing machine's operations by means of coded instructions, stored in the machine's "<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_memory" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>memory</strong></a>") was born to&nbsp;<strong>Julius Mathison</strong>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<strong>Ethel Sara</strong>&nbsp;on June 23, 1912, in&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paddington" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Paddington</a>, London. Julius was employed with the&nbsp;<strong>'</strong><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_Civil_Service_(British_India)" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>Indian Civil Service</strong></a><strong>'</strong>, and with his wife, he had another son, John. Just before he was born two tragedies had happened:&nbsp;<strong>Captain Scott's Antarctic expedition&nbsp;</strong>and the&nbsp;<strong>sinking of the Titanic&nbsp;</strong>on its maiden voyage across the Atlantic.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Discouraged at school</strong></li>
</ul>
<p>Alan spent much of his early life separated from his parents, as his father worked in the British administration of India. He pursued his elementary&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Education" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">education</a>&nbsp;from&nbsp;<strong>'St Michael's',&nbsp;</strong>later educated at&nbsp;<strong>Hazelhurst Preparatory School</strong>, an independent school in the village of Frant in&nbsp;<strong>Sussex&nbsp;</strong>(now&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Sussex" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">East Sussex</a>). In 1926, at the age of 13, he went on to&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sherborne_School" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>Sherborne School</strong></a>, a classic English public school in the market town of&nbsp;<strong>Sherborne&nbsp;</strong>in Dorset. His teachers noticed he had an ingrained talent for math. But they found faults in how he expressed that ability. While they agreed he had a "distinct talent" for the subject, they also said that he didn't&nbsp;know how to make an "<strong>intelligible and legible</strong>" solution so people could understand it. Some of the academic staff at the school did not approve of Turing's inclination for mathematics and science as they considered classical studies to be more valuable. They even tried to discourage him from pursuing science. In fact, the&nbsp;<strong>headmaster&nbsp;</strong>wrote a letter to his parents discussing his situation that:</p>
<p><em>"If he is to be solely a Scientific Specialist, he is wasting his time at a Public School."</em></p>
<p>However, it did not discourage Turing from studying what attracted his interest. He solved complex problems without even studying&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_calculus" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>elementary calculus</strong></a><strong>&nbsp;</strong>and he also studied advanced modern scientific ideas, such as&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_relativity" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">relativity</a>, on his own, running far ahead of the school syllabus.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>A new home in Cambridge</strong></li>
</ul>
<p><strong></strong>After&nbsp;<strong>Sherborne</strong>, Turing (a founding father of&nbsp;<strong>artificial intelligence</strong>&nbsp;and of modern&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cognitive_science" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>cognitive science</strong></a>) won a&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scholarship" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">scholarship</a>&nbsp;to&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/King%27s_College,_Cambridge" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">King's College, Cambridge</a>&nbsp;<em>&mdash;&nbsp;</em>and studied as an undergraduate from 1931 to 1934, where he was awarded first-class&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Honour" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>honour</strong></a>s in mathematics.</p>
<ul>
<li>In 1935, at the age of 22, he was elected a fellow of King's College on the strength of a&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thesis" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">dissertation</a>&nbsp;in which he proved the&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_limit_theorem" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>central limit theorem</strong></a>. Unknown to the committee, the theorem had already been proven, in 1922, by&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jarl_Waldemar_Lindeberg" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>Jarl Waldemar Lindeberg</strong></a>. Turing was already on track for a distinguished career in pure&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>mathematics</strong></a>.</li>
<li><strong>&nbsp;He Single-Handedly Blew The Lid Open On Computer Algorithms, But Did Not Live To Reap The Rewards.</strong></li>
</ul>
<p>In 1936, Turing published his paper "<strong>On Computable Numbers, with an Application to the&nbsp;</strong><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entscheidungsproblem" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>Entscheidungsproblem</strong></a>". It was published in the&nbsp;<strong>Proceedings of the London Mathematical Society journal&nbsp;</strong>in two parts, the first on 30 November and the second on 23 December. In this paper, Turing reformulated<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kurt_G%C3%B6del" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">&nbsp;<strong>Kurt G&ouml;del</strong></a><strong>'s&nbsp;</strong>1931 results on the limits of&nbsp;<strong>proof</strong>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<strong>computation</strong>, replacing G&ouml;del's universal arithmetic-based formal language with the formal and simple&nbsp;<strong>hypothetical&nbsp;</strong>devices that became known as&nbsp;<strong>'Turing machines'</strong>. These machines were capable of calculating anything that can be quantified. The modern computer came into existence because of this assumption made by the young Turing.</p>
<ul>
<li>From September 1936 to July 1938, Turing spent most of his time studying under Church at&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Princeton_University" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>Princeton University</strong></a>, in the second year as a&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jane_Eliza_Procter_Fellowship" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>Jane Eliza&nbsp;</strong>Procter</a>&nbsp;Visiting Fellow. In addition to his purely mathematical work, he studied&nbsp;<strong>cryptology</strong>&nbsp;and also built three of four stages of an electro-mechanical binary multiplier. In June 1938, he completed his PhD under&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_von_Neumann" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>von Neumann</strong></a><strong>'s&nbsp;</strong>supervision from the Department of Mathematics at&nbsp;<strong>Princeton</strong>; his dissertation,&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systems_of_Logic_Based_on_Ordinals" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>Systems of Logic Based on Ordinals</strong></a>, introduced the concept of ordinal logic and the notion of relative computing&nbsp;<em>&mdash;&nbsp;</em>played a significant role in introducing the ordinal logic.&nbsp;<strong>John von Neumann&nbsp;</strong>wanted to hire him as his postdoctoral assistant, but he went back to&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambridge" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Cambridge</a>&nbsp;<em>&mdash;&nbsp;</em>a year later Turing was involved in&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">war</a>&nbsp;work at Bletchley Park.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>&nbsp;</strong></p>
<p><strong>Career</strong></p>
<p>________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________</p>
<ul>
<li>&nbsp;After two years at Princeton, developing ideas about&nbsp;<strong>secret ciphers</strong>, Turing (who invented the idea of a "<strong>Universal Machine</strong>" that could decode and perform any set of instructions) returned to Britain and took up a part-time job at the&nbsp;<strong>'Government Code and Cypher School</strong>' ('GC &amp; CS'), an&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organization" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">organization&nbsp;</a>that specialized in breaking war codes. Even with&nbsp;<strong>12,000 people</strong>&nbsp;working three shifts around the clock at the British secret code deciphering facility at&nbsp;<strong>Bletchley Par</strong>k, no one could break it. That's where Turing stepped in.</li>
<li><strong>His Work Saved Millions Of Lives In WWII, Just Not His Own.</strong></li>
</ul>
<p>In July 1939, the&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biuro_Szyfr%C3%B3w" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>Polish Cipher Bureau</strong></a><strong>&nbsp;</strong>passed on crucial information about the Enigma machine, which was used by the&nbsp;<strong>Germans</strong>&nbsp;to encipher all its&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">military</a>&nbsp;and naval signals. In September 1939, Turing rapidly developed a new&nbsp;<strong>electromechanical</strong>&nbsp;machine (the "<strong>Bombe</strong>") capable of breaking Enigma messages on an industrial scale. Once the Enigma machine was cracked, 211 Bombe machines were built and ran around the clock. Turing was instrumental in creating a code-breaking machine called the Bombe. By the end of the war, the Bombe figured out everything that the German navy was saying. Turing's work at Bletchley Park was vital in ending the war. General&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>Dwight D. Eisenhower</strong></a><strong>&nbsp;</strong>told the British intelligence chief in July 1945 that the work done by Turing and his colleagues at Bletchley probably shortened the war by two years. Turing had saved millions of British and American lives. Unfortunately, it was not enough to save his own.</p>
<ul>
<li>In 1948, Turing (who was never fully recognized in his home country during his lifetime due to his homosexuality, which was then a crime in the<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">&nbsp;UK</a>) began working at the&nbsp;<strong>'Computing Laboratory</strong>' initiated by mathematician&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Newman" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Max Newman</a>, located in the&nbsp;<strong>'University of Manchester'</strong>. It was here that the former began showing an inclination towards mathematical biology.</li>
<li>&nbsp;The same year, he also worked as a Lecturer at the University of Manchester (<strong>Department of Mathematics</strong>). During this time, with help from his friend,&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D._G._Champernowne" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>D. G. Champernowne</strong></a>, he began developing a chess program, which could be played on a computer that he had envisioned but not built.</li>
<li>&nbsp;In 1948, he also stated the&nbsp;<strong>'</strong><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LU_decomposition" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>LU decomposition</strong></a><strong>&nbsp;method'</strong>, a pioneering technique which is presently used to solve&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics)" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">matrices</a>.</li>
<li>The following year, he was promoted at the university to the post of Deputy Director of the&nbsp;<strong>'Computing Laboratory'</strong>. He developed a type of&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">software</a>&nbsp;named '<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manchester_Mark_1" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Manchester Mark 1</a>', while continuing to research on abstract mathematics and artificial intelligence.</li>
<li><strong>The Turing Test Is Still The Ultimate Computering AI Test</strong></li>
</ul>
<p>In 1950, Turing came up with an important question:&nbsp;<strong>Can machines think?</strong>&nbsp;He developed the&nbsp;<strong>'Turing Test'&nbsp;</strong><em>&mdash;&nbsp;</em>a way to test a&nbsp;<strong>machine's ability&nbsp;</strong>to demonstrate intelligence. The test is simple. A judge has a conversation over a teletype with a human and a machine, both of which he cannot see. If the judge cannot tell reliably which is which, the machine passes the test. The idea kicked off the study of&nbsp;<strong>artificial intelligence&nbsp;</strong><em>&mdash;&nbsp;</em>and remains his best known work and was a key contribution to the field of Artificial Intelligence.</p>
<ul>
<li>When Turing was 39 years old in 1951, he turned to mathematical biology, finally published his masterpiece "<strong>The Chemical Basis of Morphogenesis</strong>" in January 1952. He also predicted oscillating chemical reactions such as the&nbsp;<strong>Belousov&ndash;Zhabotinsky reaction</strong>, first observed in the 1960s.</li>
</ul>

<p><strong>Personal Life &amp; Legacy</strong></p>
<p>________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________</p>
<p>&nbsp;While studying at the&nbsp;<strong>'Sherborne School'</strong>, Alan Turing fell in love with befriended classmate at Sherborne &ndash; a boy called&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alan_Turing#Christopher_Morcom" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>Christopher Morcom</strong></a>. It was hopeless, unrequited love. The blossoming friendship abruptly ended when&nbsp;<strong>Morcom</strong>&nbsp;died of&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mycobacterium_bovis" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">bovine tuberculosis</a>.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Devastated but inspired by his friend's death</strong></li>
</ul>
<p>The event caused Turing&nbsp;<strong>great sorrow</strong>. He coped with his grief by working that much harder on the topics of science and mathematics that he had shared with Morcom. In a letter to&nbsp;<strong>Morcom's mother&nbsp;</strong>Turing said:</p>
<p><em>"I am sure I could not have found anywhere another companion so brilliant and yet so charming and unconceited. I regarded my interest in my work, and in such things as astronomy (to which he introduced me) as something to be shared with him and I think he felt a little the same about me</em></p>
<p><em>... I know I must put as much energy if not as much interest into my work as if he were alive, because that is what he would like me to"</em></p>
<p>A day before the third anniversary of&nbsp;<strong>Morcom's death&nbsp;</strong>(12 February, 1933), Alan wrote to&nbsp;<strong>Morcom's mother</strong>:</p>
<p><em>"I expect you will be thinking of Chris when this reaches you. I shall too, and this letter is just to tell you that I shall be thinking of Chris and of you tomorrow. I am sure that he is as happy now as he was when he was here. Your affectionate"</em></p>
<p>Morcom's death was the cause of Turing's&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atheism" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">atheism</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Materialism" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">materialism.</a>&nbsp;Turing wanted to believe that Christopher's mind somehow lived on. His emotional turmoil involved a scientific fascination with the problem of mind and brain. In a later letter, also written to&nbsp;<strong>Morcom's mother</strong>, Turing said:</p>
<p><em>"Personally, I believe that spirit is really eternally connected with matter but certainly not by the same kind of body ... as regards the actual connection between spirit and body I consider that the body can hold on to a 'spirit', whilst the body is alive and awake the two are firmly connected. When the body is asleep I cannot guess what happens but when the body dies, the 'mechanism' of the body, holding the spirit is gone and the spirit finds a new body sooner or later, perhaps"</em></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>End of a brief engagement</strong></li>
</ul>
<p>In 1941&nbsp;<em>&mdash;&nbsp;</em>in the course of the exciting work of mastering the&nbsp;<strong>German submarine communication system&nbsp;</strong><em>&mdash;&nbsp;</em>Turing had found himself attracted to a young mathematician and Bletchley cryptanalyst called&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joan_Clarke" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>Joan Clarke</strong></a>. He proposed to her, but immediately told&nbsp;her of his "<strong>homosexual tendencies</strong>", and the engagement soon ended. After this, he became more confident in developing his homosexual life.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Convicted for gross indecency</strong></li>
</ul>
<p>All&nbsp;<strong>male homosexual activity</strong>&nbsp;was illegal until 1967 and&nbsp;<em>&mdash;&nbsp;</em>at the age of 39, Alan got into a affair with 19 years old young Manchester man called&nbsp;<strong>Arnold Murray</strong>. During a burglary investigation, personal details about his homosexuality came into light, and Alan was arrested on charges of indecency. When Turing was arrested for being gay, he couldn't tell&nbsp;the cops that he was the "<strong>savior of England</strong>," the guy who&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winston_Churchill" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>Winston Churchill</strong></a><strong>&nbsp;</strong>had said&nbsp;<strong>made the single biggest contribution to ending the war</strong>. Instead, they just treated him like some kind of&nbsp;<strong>pervert</strong>, and that was it. Perhaps his punishment would have been less dire if he could have told people what he had achieved.</p>
<ul>
<li>After conviction in 1952, he was given an option: he could go to prison or be put on a&nbsp;<strong>treatment of female hormones</strong>. The ingenious&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">logic</a>ian chose&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medication" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">medication</a>&nbsp;so that he could continue his scientific work at home.</li>
<li><strong>He Most Likely Took His Own Life Using A Cyanide-Laced Apple</strong></li>
</ul>
<p>Unable to be himself, his thoughts turned to ending his life. On June 8, 1954, Alan was found poisoned at home, and after the&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autopsy" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>autopsy</strong></a>, it was concluded that he had taken his&nbsp;own life by consuming an apple that was laced with large quantities of&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_cyanide" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>potassium cyanide</strong></a>. Friends of Alan, along with his mother, thought he might have killed himself accidentally. They claimed he hadn't seemed depressed in the days and weeks leading up to his death. There was also the fact that he had been experimenting with cyanide in a personal&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laboratory" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">laboratory</a>before he died. Rather than think he could have used this poison to deliberately taint the apple, they thought that he had<strong>&nbsp;accidently</strong>&nbsp;gotten some on it and died.</p>
<ul>
<li>Several awards have been named after this mathematical&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genius" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">genius</a>, and many biographies have been penned, the most notable being by the&nbsp;<strong>'</strong><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Society" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>Royal Society</strong></a><strong>'</strong>.</li>
<li>A novel,&nbsp;<strong>'</strong><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryptonomicon" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>Cryptonomicon</strong></a><strong>'</strong>, by American writer,&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neal_Stephenson" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Neal Stephenson</a>, published in 1999, refers to this famous mathematician.</li>
<li>The 2014 movie,&nbsp;<strong>'</strong><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Imitation_Game" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener"><strong>The Imitation Game</strong></a><strong>'</strong>, was featured on Alan's life, with British actor&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benedict_Cumberbatch" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Benedict Cumberbatch</a>&nbsp;playing the mathematician's role.</li>
<li>&nbsp;Several universities have named rooms, buildings, and even computer programs, after the distinguished mathematician.</li>
</ul>

<p><br /><strong> Alan Turing &minus; Timeline</strong></p>
<p><br /><strong>1912 (23 June):</strong> Birth, Paddington, London</p>
<p><br /><strong>1926-31:</strong> Sherborne School</p>
<p><br /><strong>1930:</strong> Death of friend Christopher Morcom</p>
<p><br /><strong>1931-34:</strong> Undergraduate at King's College, Cambridge University</p>
<p><br /><strong>1932-35:</strong> Quantum mechanics, probability, logic. Fellow of King's College, Cambridge</p>
<p><br /><strong>1936:</strong> The Turing machine, computability, universal machine</p>
<p><br /><strong>1936-38:</strong> Princeton University. Ph.D. Logic, algebra, number theory</p>
<p><br /><strong>1938-39:</strong> Return to Cambridge. Introduced to German Enigma cipher machine</p>
<p><br /><strong>1939-40:</strong> The Bombe, machine for Enigma decryption</p>
<p><br /><strong>1939-42:</strong> Breaking of U-boat Enigma, saving battle of the Atlantic</p>
<p><br /><strong>1943-45:</strong> Chief Anglo-American crypto consultant. Electronic work.</p>
<p><br /><strong>1945:</strong> National Physical Laboratory, London</p>
<p><br /><strong>1946:</strong> Computer and software design leading the world.</p>
<p><br /><strong>1947-48:</strong> Programming, neural nets, and artificial intelligence</p>
<p><br /><strong>1948:</strong> Manchester University, first serious mathematical use of a computer</p>
<p><br /><strong>1950:</strong> The Turing Test for machine intelligence</p>
<p><br /><strong>1951:</strong> Elected FRS. Non-linear theory of biological growth</p>
<p><br /><strong>1952:</strong> Arrested as a homosexual, loss of security clearance</p>
<p><br /><strong>1953-54:</strong> Unfinished work in biology and physics</p>
<p><br /><strong>1954 (7 June):</strong> Death (suicide) by cyanide poisoning, Wilmslow, Cheshire.</p>
